\begin{abstract}
We data-mine the Advanced Sports Analytics player--tournament PGA Tour corpus distributed on Kaggle, covering 36{,}864 player--events in 333 tournaments between 2015 and 2022 ($n = 29{,}180$ with complete 4-component Strokes Gained [SG] attribution).  The study is explicitly \emph{within-tour} and \emph{descriptive-inferential} rather than \emph{elite-vs-amateur}: every player in the sample is already at the top of global golf, so we characterise which SG skills most separate the best of this elite from its median.  We (i)~distinguish the arithmetic \emph{identity} that Var$(\text{SG\_total})$ decomposes into Var$(\text{OTT})$\,+\,Var$(\text{APP})$\,+\,Var$(\text{ARG})$\,+\,Var$(\text{PUTT})$\,+\,covariances from the \emph{importance} question, ``which component contributes most to finish position?'', for which we report Lindeman/Merenda/Gold (LMG) dominance analysis --- a Shapley-value attribution of $R^2$; (ii)~complement the LMG importance numbers with 500-bootstrap-CI Cohen's $d$, Fitzsimons' continuous-$r$ back-transform, and reliability-based attenuation-corrected within-tournament standardised betas with VIFs; (iii)~show that the v1 four-archetype $k$-means solution does not exceed a matched-covariance Gaussian null on silhouette, and that the v1 claim ``19 of the top-20 players fall in one ball-striker cluster'' collapses from a 90\% concentration in an absolute-scope clustering (which re-states the SG\_total ranking because the components sum to the total) to 45\% in a residual-scope clustering that removes each player's level; (iv)~re-evaluate predictive models under a cross-validation grid (random 5-fold, GroupKFold by tournament, GroupKFold by player, out-of-time 2021--22), separate the same-tournament ``sanity-check'' cut classifier from a genuine trailing-SG classifier (AUC 0.89 vs.\ 0.63), and report Spearman rank scores for the finish regressor; (v)~formally test stationarity of within-tournament scoring dispersion and winner SG across seasons via tournament-level fixed-effects OLS with HC0 robust SEs and a 2018 Chow break test.  LMG attributes 34.0\% [32.8, 35.3] of finish-position $R^2$ to approach, 31.7\% [30.1, 33.2] to putting, 20.4\% [18.9, 22.0] to off-the-tee, and 13.9\% [12.0, 15.2] to around-the-green; attenuation correction shifts within-tournament betas by less than 4\%.  Neither scoring dispersion nor winner SG exhibits a detectable linear trend or 2018 structural break.  All code, data links, and v2 artefacts are released at \url{https://github.com/edwardxiong2027/sgmine-pga}.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} golf analytics, Strokes Gained, data mining, dominance analysis, LMG, cluster stability, GroupKFold, Chow test, PGA Tour.

\section{Introduction}

Modern professional golf analytics is dominated by the Strokes Gained (SG) framework introduced by \citet{broadie2012assessing,broadie2014every}.  By benchmarking every shot against the field-average expectation from the same starting lie, distance-to-hole, and surface type, SG decomposes raw scoring into four shot-type buckets --- \emph{off-the-tee} (OTT), \emph{approach} (APP), \emph{around-the-green} (ARG), and \emph{putting} (PUTT) --- whose sum is \emph{SG\_total}, the per-round advantage of a player over the weekly field.  Since its 2011 adoption by the PGA Tour \citep{broadie2008pga}, SG has become the lingua franca of tournament analysis.

Despite a large body of work on individual SG components \citep{fearing2011golf,hickman2019driving,lonzani2014strokes}, three methodological gaps persist when SG data are analysed at scale.  First, the ``variance decomposition of SG\_total'' reported in several recent analyses is an arithmetic identity: because SG\_total is \emph{defined} as the sum of its four components, any such decomposition attributes variance to components without ever reaching the real question of interest, namely \emph{which component contributes most to winning}?  Second, most archetype clusterings of the SG profile implicitly include the SG\_total level dimension, so the cluster with the highest centroid on the sum contains the top players by construction --- a form of analytic circularity.  Third, predictive models that use same-tournament SG to predict made-cut or finish position reproduce the definition of those outcomes up to noise; near-unity accuracy does not establish predictive skill.

The present paper (v2) was rewritten in response to a peer-review round that flagged each of the above.  We retain the primary descriptive aim of the v1 analysis --- profiling within-tour determinants of PGA Tour finish position --- but replace every analysis that conflated identity with importance, or sample-level with out-of-sample evaluation, with a methodologically honest counterpart.  Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=0pt]
\item A quality-controlled descriptive profile of 36{,}864 player-events in 2015--2022 (Section~\ref{sec:data}).
\item A \emph{clear separation} of (a)~the arithmetic identity $\mathrm{Var}(\text{SG\_total}) = \sum \mathrm{Var}(\text{SG}_c) + \text{covariances}$ from (b)~LMG \citep{lindeman1980lmg,gromping2007lmg,shapley1953value} dominance analysis of the components on finish position.  The latter is the quantity of real interest; we report 200-bootstrap 95\% CIs (Section~\ref{sec:decomp}).
\item 500-bootstrap Cohen's $d$ top-vs-bottom-decile with its Fitzsimons \citep{fitzsimons2008death} continuous-$r$ back-transform and a paired full-sample Pearson $r$ \emph{companion}; attenuation-corrected within-tournament standardised betas with VIFs (Section~\ref{sec:finish}).  The corrections shift the rank-ordering minimally, but the quantities reported now have the right interpretation.
\item A sanity-checked archetype analysis: (a)~silhouette at every $k$ is benchmarked against a matched-covariance Gaussian null (99 permutations); (b)~clustering is performed \emph{both} on the absolute SG profile (v1-equivalent) and on the per-player residual profile (level removed); (c)~cluster stability is estimated on 200 bootstrap replicates with Hungarian label matching; (d)~the v1 ``19-of-20 in one cluster'' claim is restated as a falsifiable concentration test (Section~\ref{sec:arch}).
\item Predictive models re-evaluated on a 4-point CV grid (random 5-fold, GroupKFold by tournament, GroupKFold by player, out-of-time 2021--22); the same-tournament cut classifier is relabelled as a sanity check and paired with a genuine trailing-SG model; the finish regressor reports Spearman rank scores alongside $R^2$ (Section~\ref{sec:models}).
\item Formal stationarity: linear-trend tests on annual series, tournament-level OLS with season fixed effects and HC0 robust SEs \citep{white1980robust}, and a Chow \citep{chow1960tests} structural-break test at the 2018 boundary (Section~\ref{sec:trend}).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Scope clarification.}  The word \emph{elite} in the v1 title was ambiguous: every player in the ASA sample is already at the top of global golf.  The contrast we actually study is \emph{within-tour}: what separates the best of this elite from its median.  The present title reflects that scope explicitly.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

The statistical study of PGA Tour performance dates at least to \citet{connolly2008skill}, who fit mixed-effects models separating skill from luck in tournament scoring and concluded that roughly 85\% of the between-player variance in 18-hole scores is attributable to persistent skill.  \citet{broadie2008pga,broadie2012assessing,broadie2014every} subsequently introduced Strokes Gained, anchoring every shot to a data-driven benchmark rather than to counting statistics such as fairways hit or greens in regulation.  \citet{fearing2011golf} used the same framework to show that putting accounts for roughly 15\% of scoring \emph{variance} --- specifically addressing the importance question on an outcome, which is the analogue of our LMG analysis --- and that the ``short-game versus long-game'' dichotomy exaggerated by earlier coaching literature does not survive an honest variance decomposition.  Our Section~\ref{sec:decomp} follows \citet{fearing2011golf} in separating identity from importance.

More recent work has probed individual components.  \citet{hickman2019driving} document that increases in driving distance are associated with both lower scoring and reduced accuracy, but that the scoring benefit survives controlling for accuracy.  \citet{baugher2016statistical} and \citet{lonzani2014strokes} use component-level SG to model season-long money list finishes.  Machine-learning approaches have appeared more recently: \citet{kim2024random} fit random forests to tournament outcomes, finding comparable out-of-sample $R^2$ to ours.  On competitive-balance and incentive design, \citet{spears2018scoring} track scoring dispersion and \citet{brown2011quitters} study ``superstar effects''; \citet{alexander2014price} relates purse structure to effort.

Our methodological contribution extends this literature in four directions.  First, we apply LMG dominance analysis \citep{lindeman1980lmg,gromping2007lmg,shapley1953value} rather than an identity decomposition.  Second, we report 500-bootstrap CIs and a Fitzsimons \citep{fitzsimons2008death} continuous-$r$ back-transform for the extreme-groups Cohen's $d$, so that the reported effect sizes are not inflated by dichotomisation \citep{hunter1990methods}.  Third, we benchmark every silhouette against a Gaussian null and check cluster-level claims against both absolute-scope and residual-scope clusterings, closing the circularity gap.  Fourth, we evaluate predictive models under GroupKFold (tournament, player) and out-of-time CV rather than random 5-fold alone; the difference between schemes is diagnostic of the feature--label relationship.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Source and schema}

The data come from Advanced Sports Analytics \citep{asa_pga_source} and are distributed on Kaggle \citep{kaggle_asa_pga}.  The file \texttt{ASA All PGA Raw Data -- Tourn Level.csv} contains one row per player--tournament, with columns for the player (initialised and full name), the tournament (id, name, course, date, purse, season, no-cut indicator), the round-level outcomes (strokes, par, rounds played, made-cut flag, finish position \texttt{pos}), three sets of fantasy-points columns (\texttt{DKP/FDP/SDP}), and six Strokes Gained fields (\texttt{sg\_putt}, \texttt{sg\_arg}, \texttt{sg\_app}, \texttt{sg\_ott}, \texttt{sg\_t2g}, \texttt{sg\_total}).  Dates span 12 October 2014 through 5 June 2022.

\subsection{Quality control and filtering}

The raw file contains 36{,}864 rows covering 499 unique players, 333 tournaments, and 92 courses across the 2015--2022 seasons (Table~\ref{tab:profile}).  Four fields contain the string \texttt{"NA"} which we coerce to \texttt{NaN}.  Strokes Gained attribution is present on 79.2\% of rows; all four SG components are simultaneously present on 29{,}180 rows, which becomes our primary analysis set.  Missing SG is concentrated in non-main events (e.g.\ opposite-field tournaments and a handful of 2015--2016 events in which the third-party SG pipeline was not run); we therefore treat SG-complete rows as a missing-at-random sample for unconditional analyses and flag the caveat where a season-level coverage artefact is plausible (Section~\ref{sec:trend}).

\section{Identity vs.\ Importance of SG Components}
\label{sec:decomp}

\subsection{Arithmetic identity of SG\_total}

Because SG\_total is defined as the sum of its four components, the variance identity
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:identity}
\mathrm{Var}(\text{SG\_total}) = \sum_{c \in \{\text{OTT,APP,ARG,PUTT}\}} \mathrm{Var}(\text{SG}_c) \;+\; 2\sum_{c < c'} \mathrm{Cov}(\text{SG}_c, \text{SG}_{c'})
\end{equation}
is exact up to the rounding under which individual components and \texttt{sg\_t2g} are stored.  A regression of SG\_total on the four components therefore attains $R^2 \approx 0.986$ with coefficients near unity, and the ``unique $R^2$ when each component is removed'' simply reads off the marginal variance share (with adjustment for covariances) in (\ref{eq:identity}).  For transparency, we report the marginal variance shares in the left panel of Figure~\ref{fig:decomp}: approach (34.0\%) and putting (34.0\%) carry approximately twice the share of off-the-tee (17.7\%) or around-the-green (14.3\%).  \textbf{This is a property of the SG metric's distribution, not a claim about component importance on winning.}  The reviewer correctly identified that reporting (\ref{eq:identity}) without the LMG companion in v1 conflated the two.

\subsection{LMG dominance analysis on finish position}

The substantive question is: holding the sum constant, which component best explains variation in finish position?  We fit an OLS regression of $-\text{pos}$ (sign flipped so positive means a better finish) on the four within-tournament standardised SG components and perform Lindeman/Merenda/Gold dominance analysis \citep{lindeman1980lmg,gromping2007lmg}: each component's share is the mean incremental $R^2$ over all $4! = 24$ orderings, equivalently its Shapley value \citep{shapley1953value} on the $R^2$ game.  Uncertainty is estimated by 200 resampling bootstraps.

\section{What Predicts Finish Position Within a Tournament?}
\label{sec:finish}

\subsection{Extreme-groups and continuous effect sizes}

We compute, among the 17{,}048 cut-making rows, Cohen's $d$ between the top-decile ($\text{pos} \le 6$) and bottom-decile ($\text{pos} \ge 64$) cut-makers with 500-bootstrap 95\% CIs, together with the Fitzsimons' \citep{fitzsimons2008death,hunter1990methods} continuous-$r$ back-transform $r_\text{cont} \approx d/\sqrt{d^2 + 1/p(1-p)}$ at $p = 0.1$, and the paired full-sample Pearson $r$ between the component SG and $-\text{pos}$ computed on \emph{all} cut-makers (Figure~\ref{fig:effect}a, Table~\ref{tab:effect}).  Reporting both lets the reader see the inflation of $d$ relative to the continuous effect.

The Fitzsimons back-transform and the full-sample $r$ agree within about 0.04 across components, and the rank-ordering is preserved: approach $>$ putting $>$ OTT $>$ ARG.  The extreme-groups $d$ is 20--30\% larger than the continuous-$r$ effect for every component, which is consistent with the dichotomisation inflation noted by \citet{hunter1990methods}; the paper's headline effect sizes are therefore the full-sample Pearson $r$, not $d$.

\subsection{Within-tournament standardised regression with attenuation correction and VIFs}

Marginal correlations confound across tournaments of different difficulty.  We therefore fit, for every tournament with at least 30 recorded SG finishers ($n=241$), the within-tournament OLS regression
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:within}
\widetilde{\text{pos}}_{ij} = \alpha_j + \beta^{OTT}_j \widetilde{SG}^{OTT}_{ij} + \beta^{APP}_j \widetilde{SG}^{APP}_{ij} + \beta^{ARG}_j \widetilde{SG}^{ARG}_{ij} + \beta^{PUTT}_j \widetilde{SG}^{PUTT}_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij},
\end{equation}
where tildes denote within-tournament standardisation (zero mean, unit variance), $i$ indexes player and $j$ indexes tournament.  Average $R^2$ across the 241 tournaments is 0.885 (median 0.942).  We report (i)~raw standardised betas (sign flipped so positive = better predictor of low \texttt{pos}), (ii)~the 2.5th/97.5th percentile of the per-tournament beta distribution as a 95\% CI, (iii)~reliability-based attenuation-corrected betas \citep{hunter1990methods} $\beta^*/\sqrt{r_{xx}}$ with $r_{xx} = n_\text{shots}/(n_\text{shots}+1)$ for canonical per-round shot counts $(n_\text{OTT}, n_\text{APP}, n_\text{ARG}, n_\text{PUTT}) = (14, 13, 5, 29)$, and (iv)~the mean variance inflation factor (VIF).

\section{Player ``Archetypes'': Sanity Checks}
\label{sec:arch}

The v1 submission reported a four-cluster $k$-means solution on the career-mean SG profile (silhouette 0.205) and claimed that 19 of the top-20 players by career SG\_total fell in a single ``ball-striker'' cluster.  The peer reviewer observed three methodological issues with this finding, each of which we address below.

\subsection{Silhouette vs Gaussian null}

A silhouette of 0.205 is below the \citet{rousseeuw1987silhouettes} rule-of-thumb ``weak structure'' threshold of 0.25.  We therefore compare the observed silhouette at every $k \in \{2, \dots, 8\}$ against the 95th percentile of a Gaussian null: 99 random samples from a multivariate normal matched to the observed mean and covariance of the standardised feature matrix, each clustered at the same $k$.  Figure~\ref{fig:arch}(a)--(b) shows the sweep for both the absolute-scope and residual-scope profiles.  In the absolute scope the observed silhouette at every $k$ is either below or within noise of the null 95th percentile --- at $k = 4$ the observed silhouette is 0.205 vs a null 95th of 0.214.  \textbf{There is no cluster structure beyond a Gaussian with matched covariance.}  The same conclusion holds for the residual scope.  We retain $k = 4$ only as a descriptive device with this caveat front-loaded.

\subsection{Absolute vs residual clustering and the circularity of the top-20 concentration}

The v1 clustering was performed on the standardised absolute components $(\text{OTT, APP, ARG, PUTT})$.  But because the components sum to SG\_total, the cluster with the highest centroid on the sum will contain the top-SG\_total players \emph{by construction}.  We therefore re-cluster on the per-player residual profile $(\text{OTT}, \text{APP}, \text{ARG}, \text{PUTT})$ with each player's mean SG level subtracted before standardisation.  Figure~\ref{fig:arch}(c) shows top-20 concentration under both clusterings: 18 of the top-20 fall in a single absolute-scope cluster (90\%), but only 9 fall in the most-concentrated residual-scope cluster (45\%, with the next 8 in a different residual cluster).  \textbf{The v1 ``19 of 20 in one ball-striker cluster'' claim is largely an artefact of the level dimension.}  Once level is removed, the elite are distributed across shape-based archetypes rather than concentrated in one.

\subsection{Bootstrap cluster stability}

To quantify which cluster assignments are reliable we refit $k$-means on 200 bootstrap samples of the player set at $k=4$, match bootstrap cluster IDs to the reference via the Hungarian algorithm on shared players, and compute a per-player agreement rate: the fraction of bootstraps in which the player falls into the same reference cluster.  Mean per-player agreement is 0.76 for both scopes; 70\% of players are above the 0.70 threshold in absolute scope and 64\% in residual scope.  We regard individual assignments with stability $< 0.70$ as noise and carry the \texttt{stability\_abs}, \texttt{stability\_res} columns in \texttt{player\_clusters.csv} for downstream users.

\section{Cross-Validated Predictive Models}
\label{sec:models}

Two supervised models are fit.  In v1 they were evaluated under random 5-fold CV alone.  The peer reviewer noted that (i)~random 5-fold may leak tournament and player identity, and (ii)~predicting \texttt{made\_cut} from same-tournament SG is a near-arithmetic identity rather than a predictive achievement.  We address both points.

\paragraph{CV grid.}  Every model is evaluated under four schemes:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=0pt]
\item \textbf{Random 5-fold} --- for comparability with v1.
\item \textbf{GroupKFold by tournament} (5 splits) --- no tournament appears in both train and test.
\item \textbf{GroupKFold by player} (5 splits) --- no player appears in both train and test.
\item \textbf{Out-of-time 2021--22} --- trained on 2015--20, evaluated on 2021--22.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Sanity-check cut classifier (same-tournament SG).}  Target \texttt{made\_cut} on all 29{,}180 rows (58.4\% positive baseline), features = the four same-tournament SG components.  Because the cut line is a transformation of cumulative strokes and SG is the complement of strokes against a field benchmark, predicting the cut from same-tournament SG is near-tautological.  The purpose of reporting this model is not predictive achievement but verification of SG attribution.

\paragraph{Genuine cut classifier (trailing SG).}  Same target, but features are each player's mean SG components over the \emph{immediately preceding} 10 events (\texttt{shift(1)} + rolling mean, strictly no leakage) together with the count of prior events.  This is a real predictive question: does recent form predict next week's cut?

\paragraph{Finish regressor.}  Continuous target \texttt{pos} on the 16{,}291-row cut-making subset; features = same-tournament SG.  We report Ridge (L2) and Random Forest under all four CV schemes and both $R^2$ and MAE, plus Spearman rank correlation for the random 5-fold.  This model is also identity-dominated --- finish is a rank of strokes, and SG is a normalisation of the same strokes --- so we present it alongside the sanity framing of the cut classifier.

\section{Temporal Trends: Formal Stationarity Tests}
\label{sec:trend}

Has within-tour scoring differentiation shifted over 2015--2022?  Figure~\ref{fig:trends} plots the winner mean SG\_total and the within-tournament SG\_total standard deviation by season, with linear-trend fits overlaid.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{figures/fig6_trends.pdf}
\caption{Winner mean SG\_total (a) and within-tournament SG\_total standard deviation (b) by season, with linear-trend fits, Chow structural-break $F$-statistics at the 2018 boundary, and season fixed-effects joint-$F$ tests overlaid.}
\label{fig:trends}
\end{figure}

We perform three formal tests:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=0pt]
\item \textbf{Linear trend} on the 8 annual means.  Winner SG\_total: slope = $+0.001$ per season, $p = 0.96$.  Within-tournament SD: slope = $-0.004$ per season, $p = 0.42$.
\item \textbf{Season fixed-effects OLS} on the tournament-level panel ($n = 241$ tournaments with computable within-SD, $n = 248$ tournaments with a single winner on file) with HC0 robust SEs \citep{white1980robust} and a joint $F$-test for ``all 7 season dummies = 0''.  Within-tournament SD: $F(7, 239) = 0.67$, $p = 0.70$.  Winner SG: $F(7, 234) = 0.75$, $p = 0.63$.
\item \textbf{Chow structural-break test} \citep{chow1960tests} with a 2018 boundary, motivated by the v1 narrative that pre-2018 SG coverage was lower.  Winner SG: $F = 0.71$, $p = 0.54$.  Within-tournament SD: $F = 1.07$, $p = 0.43$.
\end{enumerate}

All three tests decisively fail to reject stationarity.  Combined with the coverage statistics in Table~\ref{tab:cov} below, which show that 2015--2016 had slightly reduced SG coverage per event (which was the v1-flagged artefact) but that winner and dispersion statistics on the SG-complete subsample are still stable, the evidence is against a competitive-compression or bomb-and-gouge-era narrative at the scoring-dispersion level: 2015--2022 was effectively a structurally stable period.

\section{Discussion}

Relative to the v1 submission, three claims have been weakened and one has been strengthened.

\paragraph{Weakened: component importance is close to tied at the within-tournament level.}  LMG dominance analysis attributes 34\% of finish-position $R^2$ to approach, 32\% to putting, 20\% to OTT, and 14\% to ARG, which is tighter than the v1 ``APP $\approx$ PUTT, each 50\% larger than OTT'' headline.  The attenuation-corrected within-tournament betas place APP and PUTT within 0.001 of each other; the LMG analysis gives APP a clear edge, but the 95\% CIs overlap at the bottom of APP with the top of PUTT.  We therefore report the finding as ``APP and PUTT are the two dominant components, in that order, with the difference close to the 95\%-CI margin.''

\paragraph{Weakened: the ``ball-striker archetype'' claim was largely circular.}  $k=4$ silhouette (0.205) does not exceed a matched-covariance Gaussian null; once the SG\_total level dimension is removed by residualising within player, the top-20 concentration drops from 90\% to 45\%.  The best remaining reading of the archetype analysis is as a descriptive summary of the shape-based heterogeneity within the elite, with honest stability numbers.

\paragraph{Weakened: the cut classifier's AUC of 0.89 is a sanity check, not a predictive achievement.}  An honest trailing-SG cut classifier achieves AUC 0.63 under any CV scheme, which is what recent form is actually worth for the next-week made-cut question.

\paragraph{Strengthened: stationarity of within-tour scoring dispersion and winner SG 2015--2022.}  v1 argued for stationarity from visual inspection; v2 supplies formal linear-trend tests, a season fixed-effects joint $F$-test with HC0 SEs, and a 2018 Chow break test, each of which decisively fails to reject stationarity.

The coaching aphorism ``you drive for show and putt for dough'' remains unsupported: off-the-tee is materially below approach and putting in importance, but around-the-green is the smallest contributor, which is opposite to the aphorism's implied ranking.  This is consistent with \citet{fearing2011golf} and \citet{broadie2012assessing}.  The top-heavy concentration in the absolute-scope ball-striker cluster is a descriptive fact about the elite of this era \emph{conditional on having high SG\_total}, not an independent signal that distinguishing ``ball-strikers'' from ``short-game specialists'' carves the elite at a joint --- because, once level is removed, the elite are shape-diverse.

A methodological takeaway is the importance of (a)~distinguishing the arithmetic identity of an additive metric from the importance question on an outcome, and (b)~checking circularity: if the features used for clustering include a dimension that re-states the ranking, the cluster that contains the top of the ranking is not a discovery.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

Our analysis has several limitations.  First, SG attribution is missing on 20.8\% of player-events, disproportionately at non-main tournaments; the LMG coefficients may be modestly biased if the missing rows are systematically harder to predict.  Second, we do not incorporate course fixed effects; courses with wide fairways may weight OTT differently from courses with extreme rough, and a hierarchical model could make these heterogeneous effects explicit.  Third, the archetype analysis is reported as a descriptive exercise not supported by a Gaussian null, and would benefit from an archetypal-analysis model \citep{cutler1994archetypal} that recovers convex exemplars rather than centroids.  Fourth, our predictive models use only SG inputs and no player or tournament identifiers, by design; our interest was in the isolated contribution of the 4-component SG vector, not in building a betting model.  Future work will incorporate course characteristics, weather, and strength-of-field to push the trailing-SG cut-making AUC past 0.70.

\section{Conclusion}

Mining the ASA Advanced Sports Analytics corpus of 36{,}864 PGA Tour player-tournaments over 2015--2022, we find that (i)~the arithmetic identity decomposition of SG\_total (approach 34\%, putting 34\%, OTT 18\%, ARG 14\% of variance) is distinct from LMG dominance importance on finish position (approach 34\% [32.8, 35.3], putting 32\% [30.1, 33.2], OTT 20\% [18.9, 22.0], ARG 14\% [12.0, 15.2]); (ii)~attenuation-corrected within-tournament standardised betas place APP and PUTT within 0.001 of each other with mean VIF $<$ 1.2; (iii)~$k$-means on the absolute SG profile does not exceed a Gaussian null on silhouette, and the 90\% top-20 concentration in one ``ball-striker'' cluster collapses to 45\% when the SG\_total level dimension is removed; (iv)~a same-tournament cut classifier achieves AUC 0.89 across all CV schemes (a sanity check for SG attribution, not a predictive finding), while a genuine trailing-SG cut classifier achieves AUC 0.63 that recovers the honest value of recent form; and (v)~neither winner SG nor within-tournament scoring dispersion exhibits a detectable linear trend or a 2018 structural break.
